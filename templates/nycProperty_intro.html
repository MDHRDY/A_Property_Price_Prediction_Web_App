City Property

Location, location, location

We've all heard it, but there is some often-unexplored nuance to this dictum. In the suburbs, good schools will lift the value of every home in a school district, but in cities, location often acts on smaller scales. A subway line, for example, confers significantly more value to an apartment four blocks away than to one that is fifteen. If the same subway line were above ground, however, it could depreciate the value of nearby apartments (Oh, the noise! Shut those curtains . . .) that are only steps away.

The significance of such hyper-local effects (proximity to parks, restaurants, shops, etc.) has been glossed over by traditional metrics that often average values over large areas such as a neighborhoods, counties, or ZIP Codes. To understand such small-scale influences, many additional details might need to be factored into the standard calculations, and, presumably, this was unfeasible in the past.

Using machine learning, however, we can take many more variables into account and begin to get a more granular view of property values in dense, urban areas, where two properties that are only a five-minute walk apart might have significantly different values. The machine-learning app below translates GPS coordinates, sales dates and property sales into a property price-prediction tool.

Better than Zillow?

Not quite - but soon! Zillow has been at it for a decade, and their 'Zestimate' is the best freely-available, public-facing price estimator on the Internet. Upon initial inspection, the accuracy of their model appears quite impressive: the model predicts the value of an NYC property within 20% of the actual market value 80.7% of the time. When looking at the performance of individual boroughs however, the Zestimater somehow never performs quite as well. In fact, Zillow reports their most accurate NYC prediction is in Manhattan, where the prediction is within 20% of the actual market value only 76% of the time.

The Zestimator consistently makes predictions for less than 90% of the properties listed on Zillow's own website (which contains a fraction of the total number of properties), and these absent predictions for the properties on Zillow's site are not included in their accuracy calculation. A disinterested observer might be forgiven for suspecting a certain selectivity in reporting.

It's understandable that Zillow wants to present their model in the rosiest light. And they have! For example, if the Zestimator is quite bad for a particular region, as it is for the borough of Staten Island (see Richmond County under their New York reportings), Zillow has decided to not report the accuracy of their model in this borough.

In accounting for these details, a more plausible assessment of Zillow's Zestimator is that it predicts a value to within 20% of the actual market value of a property in New York City somewhere between 50% and 60% of the time. What's the accuracy of the model on this site? At the moment it's within 20% of the actual sales price 50% of the time. There's definitely room for improvement, but this prediction is actually quite good considering that it currently only accounts for a saleâ€™s location and sales date. These two features can account for 76% of the variation in sales prices for one- to three-unit residential properties in New York City, and presumably additional features ( such as square footage or the type of property, like a coop, condo, or brownstone), will yield even better results.

Merging these datasets results in a wealth of information, including over 1.2 million property sales.

From the links above, we see the number of transactions in Staten Island and the Bronx is indeed lower than the other boroughs, corroborating the heatmap. The number of sales transactions per year in each of the five boroughs is still lower than it was a decade ago, though all boroughs have increased sales numbers since 2010, from a low of a 24%-increase in Queens to a high of a 44%-increase in Manhattan.

Though the volume in sales has not yet matched volume from a decade ago, the median sales price of one- to three-unit residential properties has more than recovered in Manhattan and Brooklyn, where property values are now at all time highs. Queens, Staten Island and the Bronx however, are still depressed.

These summary statistics provide a cursory glance of NYC property trends, but we'd also like to see changes over time currently happening inside each borough. New York City's Department of Finance assesses the value of each NYC property on a yearly basis for purposes of taxation, and we can look at changes in assessed values to add an additional dimension.

The dataset below highlights properties that have depreciated in value by more than 60% over the last six years (blue points) or appreciated in value by more than 60% over the same period (red points). The absence of blue points in Manhattan further supports a picture of a robust market where high values continue to push higher (if the map fails to function properly, please refresh).

The figure also points to more interesting changes occurring in the outer boroughs, where the prices of properties in close proximity seem to be moving in opposite directions. Why would some properties depreciate in value by more than 60% while other properties on the same city block simultaneously appreciate in value by more than 60%? It's possible that relying on assessed values determined by NYC's Department of Finance is a mistake, since . . . . Another possibility is that since the outer boroughs have been slower to recover, the figure highlights a type of market inflection point: sales are picking up in depressed neighborhoods that have bottomed out and values are now rising. If this hypothesis is true, then neighborhoods with a higher ratio of blue points to red points (such as the Rockaways and beachfront property along Staten Island and Brooklyn) might provide interesting investment opportunities or bargains for home buyers.

An even better route for additional analysis would avoid making arbitrary cutoffs (such as observing only properties that have increased or decreased by a certain percentage) and instead build a predictive model with all of the data at hand.

With a machine learning algorithm, we can obtain a prediction for every property in the city, irrespective of whether that property has recently sold. The algorithm used here (k-NN) recognizes patterns through an instance-based learning process and outputs a property-value prediction based upon recent, nearby sales.

Future directions: the goal is to incorporate a few additional features and further develop a model that clearly out-performs Zillow's Zestimator. The performance of this model can be better benchmarked against Zillow's by tracking new sales and comparing new sales prices to each model's prediction. Additionally, users will be provided with additional tools, such as query-based, realtime real estate listings.
